Analysing the European Election: Looking at the Candidates

Estimated reading time: 11 min

May 21, 2019 #R #European Election 2019 #Election

The European Election is coming up and for the first time I have the impression this election is actually talked about and might have an impact. I don’t remember people caring that much about European Elections in the years before, but this of course could also just be because I got more interested in European politics. Unfortunately, European politics are complex and this is also mirrored in the quantity of parties that are up for vote in Germany. In Germany alone, there are 40 parties on the ballot paper and in total 1.380 candidates are trying to get one of the 96 seats allocated for Germany. No joke, my ballot paper was almost as long as I’m tall! Reading through some of the names on the ballot paper, I thought it might be interesting to do a small data analysis and to compare some of the parties by their candidate composition.

Conveniently, the data is provided by the government in a csv-file. Almost all parties have the same candidate list for all of Germany, the only party that has differing lists for each state is CDU/CSU. Depending on the analysis, I sometimes collapse CDU and CSU to a single party or only consider their liste for Berlin.

How many candidates are in a party?

While the ballot paper only shows the top ten candidates for each party (or less if the party has less), many of them have more candidates. Interestingly, some parties have ambitiously many:

df %>%
  group_by(Gruppenname) %>%
  summarise(n=n(), 
            isReplacement = sum(Kennzeichen == "Ersatzbewerber")) %>%
  filter(n >= 96) %>% arrange(-n) %>% kable()
Gruppenname n isReplacement
CDU 200 20
FDP 168 0
SPD 152 56
Die PARTEI 143 0
ÖDP 96 0

The FDP (the economic liberal party) and the CDU (conservative party) nominate both almost twice as many candidates as there are seats avaiable! The SPD (social democratic party) only has more candidates than seats since it has many replacement candidates. Die PARTEI is a satire party, so I’m not really surprised they show up with too many candidates. ÖDP, a small ecological party that never got elected into the Bundestag, also seems rather ambitious with 96 candidates. Looking them up, I found out the ÖDP does has one seat in the current parliament.

A gender-balanced parliament?

Right now, about a third of the members of european parliament (MEPs) are women. We can check the data on how good the chances are that the new parliament might sport more women (at least on the German seats). Since we’ve seen before that some parties have many more candidates than seats available, it might be misleading to look at all candidates. Instead, it gives a better picture to look at the “Spitzenkandidaten”, that is the leading candidates

top <- df %>%
  filter( Listenplatz <= 10 & Kennzeichen == "Bewerber") %>%
  ggplot(aes(x=Geschlecht, fill=Geschlecht)) + 
  geom_bar(aes(y=..prop.., group=1, 
               fill=factor(..x..)), stat="count", show.legend = F) +
  scale_y_continuous(labels = percent, name="", breaks = c(0, 0.38, 0.5, 0.62),
                     limits=c(0, 0.7)) +
  scale_x_discrete(labels=c("Male", "Female"), name="") +
  labs(subtitle="Top Candidates", title="How gender-balanced is the candidate pool?\n")
plot_grid(top, all)

The top candidates have a slightly more equal gender-ratio but nevertheless the gender ratio in the candidates seems the same as the ratio in the old parliament. However, there are 40 parties and all of them have 5 to 10 top candidates but the top candidates of the big party surely have better chances of entering the parliament. It is thus more reasonable to look at the gender ratio for each party. Since the bigger parties have good chances to get more than the top ten candidates in the parliament, we will look at the whole candidate pool for these parties. Also, I’ve pooled the parties CDU and CSU together for this plot:

Die Linke, the left party, has exactly 50% female and male candidates, wheras the Green (GRÜNE) party and the SPD have a very slight female bias. All three parties are rather left-leaning. The other three bigger parties, all more conservative, have a male bias that is especially strong for the FDP and AfD. The AfD is far-right so I’m not surprised by their poor gender ratio but I didn’t expect the FDP to do so poorly as well. I expected them be more on the same line as CDU/CSU, around 40% of females.

Having seen the gender ratio of the six main parties, it would be interesting to check which parties show the most extreme gender ratio. I classified gender ratios as extreme if they were above 75% or below 25%.

The feminist party DIE FRAUEN has only female candidates but this isn’t really surprising given their name. The only other party with a rather extreme female bias is the animal protection party TIERSCHUTZ hier!. The list of parties with an extreme male bias is comparable long: 15 parties, so more than a third of all parties on the ballot, have less than 25% female candidates. There are the right-leaning to far-right parties: III. Weg, AfD and DIE RECHTE (interestingly, the neo-Nazi party NPD is not listed here and thus has a better gender ratio than for example the FDP). The list further includes many small conservative parties such as ÖDP (ecological party), BP (bavaria party) and Bündnis C (christian party). I found especially curious the family party FAMILIE: 0 female candidates, draw your own conclusion what image they have of a family. The parties SGP (socialist equality party) and BIG (party for innovation and equity/fairness) have equality or equity in their name but it doesn’t seem to apply to gender equality. The internet party PIRATEN apparently follows the nerd stereotype with mostly male candidates.

What are the candidates working?

The data provides the title of each candidate so we can easily check which party has the highest percentage of candidates with a doctorate:

df %>%
  filter(Kennzeichen == "Bewerber" &
           Listenplatz <= 10) %>%
  group_by(Gruppenname) %>%
  summarise(titel = sum(!is.na(Titel)), n=n(), prop = titel/n,p=percent(prop)) %>%
  arrange(-prop) %>%
  select(party=Gruppenname, percentage=p, prop) %>%
  head(10) %>% mutate(party = fct_reorder(party, prop)) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x=party, y=prop)) +
  geom_bar(stat = "identity", fill="#377EB8") +
  scale_y_continuous(label=percent) +
  labs(y="", x="", title="Parties with the highest proportion of \nTop Candidates with a Doctorate") +
  coord_flip()

The far-right party AfD has with 50% the highest percentage of doctorates among the candidates, followed by the party LKR, which is actually a split from the AfD (albeit less right). Overall, 8.5% of all candidates have a doctoral degree, while in the general population only about 8% have even any university degree and only 1% have a doctoral degree.

While the data also provides the job title for each candidate, they’re not standardized so it would be more difficult to analyze. Luckily, on top of the job title, the data also provides us with a job key which indicates the area in which the candidate is working. It took me a while to find a mapping from the job index number to a description but eventually found one provided by the stats department of the Arbeitsamt.

df %>%
  filter(Listenplatz <= 10 &
           Kennzeichen == "Bewerber") %>%
  mutate(job_key = fct_lump(Berufsschluessel, n=10) %>%
           fct_infreq() %>%
           fct_recode(!!!job_dict)) %>%
  filter(job_key != "Other") %>%
  group_by(job_key) %>%
  summarize(n=n()) %>% 
  mutate(prop = n/sum(n), job_key=fct_reorder(job_key, n)) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x=job_key, y=prop)) +
  geom_bar(stat="identity",show.legend = F, fill="#377EB8") +
  scale_y_continuous(labels = percent, name="") +
  labs(x="", title="Where do most Top Candidates work?") +
  coord_flip()

About one third of candidates work in Business Management and Administration which makes the single largest area of employment. One thing to note here is that the job key doesn’t provide information if someone works as a CEO or low-level employee, it really only gives the area. The only exception to this is the category Students. By definition, these candidates are right now in education about to earn either a high-school diploma, a bacheor’s degree or a PhD. Also, students is the sixth largest group, closley followed by pensioners, both of which are group I didn’t expect to be that well reprented in the candidate pool.

Generation change

There are a few clichees out there regarding politics and age: Young people don’t care about politics (there are many polls showing young people vote at a lesser rate than older people). Politicians are always old people. While this data set is not the right one to answer how much truth there is to these stereotypes, we can check to some degree if young people aspire to become full-time politicians:

df <- df %>%
  mutate(age = year(today())- Geburtjahr)

df %>%
  ggplot(aes(x=age)) +
  geom_histogram(aes(y=..density..), binwidth = 1, alpha=0.4, fill="#377EB8") +
  stat_density(geom="line", col="#377EB8", size=1) +
  scale_x_continuous(breaks = seq(20, 80, by=10), name="") +
  scale_y_continuous(breaks=NULL, name = "") +
  labs(title="Are all politicians old?")

Interestingly, the age distribution is bimodal, that is, we have a peak at 30 years old and one at 55 years old. Of course not all candidates are elected into the parliament and most likely rookie candidates are more often delegated to later places in the list. So we shouldn’t expect the elected parliament to have a similar age distribution. But this figure is at least an indicator that young people (if you consider people around 25 still young) are interested and especially also active in politics.

df %>%
  mutate(Gruppenname = fct_reorder(Gruppenname, age),
         med_age = median(age),
         perc25 = quantile(age, prob=0.33),
         perc75 = quantile(age, prob=0.66)) %>%
  group_by(Gruppenname) %>%
  summarise(med = median(age), med_age = median(med_age), perc25=median(perc25), perc75=median(perc75) ) %>%
  filter(med <= perc25 | med >= perc75) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x=med, y=Gruppenname)) +
  geom_vline(aes(xintercept = med_age), col="grey") +
  geom_point(height=1) +
  scale_x_continuous(breaks = c(30, 40, 46, 50, 60)) +
  coord_fixed(ratio=1.5) +
  labs(x="Median Age", y="", title="Which are the youngest and oldest parties?")

The party Volksabstimmung (Democracy by referendum, they used to be far-right) has a median age of 65 years, meaning most of its candidates are older than this. It is even older than the party Graue Panther, a party for pensioners. On the other side is the party DIE DIREKTE that similarly to the party Volksabstimmung supports direct democracy (without any far-right history though) with a median age of 25. The latter party originated from a student initiative and almost all its candidates are students. Surprisingly, the conservative CSU party that probably for many invokes the image of an old, white male politician has a median age that places it in the younger parties, right below Volt.

Where do you come from?

The data also provides us with the birth place for each candidate which gives us the opportunity to check how many candidates were born in a country other than Germany. Unfortunately, the birth place only names the city but not the country. To obtain the country, I used the Google Geocoding API. Only three cities could not be identified by Googles API which was easy to code by hand.

library(ggmap)

get_country <- function(result) {
  for (comp in result$address_components) {
    if (comp$types[[1]] == "country")
      return (comp$long_name)
  }
}

locs <- unique(df$Geburtsort)
res <- purrr::map_chr(locs, possibly(
                         function(loc) {
                           geocode(loc, output = "all", force=F,
                                   inject=c("region"="de"))$results[[1]] %>%
                             get_country() }, otherwise = NA_character_ ) )

countries <- res %>% transpose() %>% simplify_all() %>% first %>% as.character()

country_mapping <- tibble(
  city = locs,
  country = na_if(countries,"NULL")
) %>%
  mutate(country = case_when(city == "Spizewka" ~ "Russia",
                             city == "Alpen" ~ "Germany",
                             city == "Babone, Kamerum" ~ "Cameroon",
                             TRUE ~ country))

Not very surprisingly, most candidates were born in Gemany:

df <- df %>%
  left_join(country_mapping, by=c("Geburtsort"="city")) 

df%>%
  count(country) %>%
  arrange(-n) %>% head(5) %>% kable

Next in line are the countries with the biggest immigrant population in Germany: Turkey, Poland and Russia. For a better comparison, I excluded Germany as country of origin from the following plot:

df %>%
  filter(country != "Germany") %>%
  mutate(country = fct_lump(country, n=10) %>%
           fct_infreq %>% fct_rev) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x=country)) +
  geom_bar(fill="#377EB8") + coord_flip() +
  labs(x="", title="In which countries were \nthe candidates born?",
       subtitle = "(Excluding Germany)\n")

Now, are some parties more diverse (in terms of birth places of their candidates) than others or are they relatively equally distributed over the parties? Let’s compare the parties by number of distinct birth countries. To make the comparison equal, I restrict to only the top candidates and also restrict the CDU to their top candidates in Berlin. This ensures that each party is represented by at most 10 candidates in this comparison:

df %>%
  filter(Listenplatz <= 10 & 
           Kennzeichen == "Bewerber" &
           Gebietsname %in% c("Bundesgebiet", "Berlin")) %>%
  group_by(Gruppenname) %>%
  summarise(n_countries=n_distinct(country), percentage=percent(n_countries/n())) %>%
  arrange(-n_countries) %>% select(-n_countries) %>% head() %>% kable
Gruppenname percentage
BIG 60.0%
DiEM25 60.0%
DIE FRAUEN 40.0%
SGP 40.0%
DIE LINKE 30.0%
MENSCHLICHE WELT 37.5%

BIG is one of the first parties in Germany founded by Muslims and has a particular focus on migrant politics. It thus comes as no surprise that they have such a high percentage of candidates born outside Germany.

Summary

While this data does not allow to directly analyse political ideology and party positions, some positions shine through in the data about the candidates. Especially for some of the smaller parties whose name I didn’t know before, I now have a better idea what kind of positions or ideology they might represent. If you’re eligible for voting, I recommend you to check the positions for the parties in more detail. And of course, don’t forget to vote.
